Canada's role as a constituent of the Second World War

Canada proved to be a major player in the Second World War, as seen by its involvements in various battles such as the Battle of Britain (1940), Battle of the Atlantic (1939-1945), the Defence of Hong Kong (1941), as well as its contributions to the Battle of Normandy. Perhaps its longest battle within the entirety of the war, the Battle of the Atlantic encompassed a 2075-day-long battle on the Atlantic against the undeniably dangerous German U-boats, which were a nickname for the infamous German submarines. The Royal Canadian Navy laid claim to over 33 destroyed enemy submarines during the course of the entire Second World War. While this number may have been a smaller quantity than the approximate 1,000 U-boats sunk by the Allies, the Canadian Navy provided a massive percentage of the total contribution to the safe passage of the nearly 25,000 ships that crossed the Atlantic during the war period.

Additionally, Canada also played a prominent role in the Battle of Britain (July 10 - October, 1940) via its presence within the air defence of Britain, against the constant air raids conducted by Germany. It is believed that over a hundred Canadians were thought to have participated in the Battle of Britain, with twenty-three who have been confirmed to have lost their lives throughout the ordeal. Three members of the Royal Canadian Air Force received the Distinguished Flying Cross for their valiant efforts during the battle, with a number of Canadians also playing an indubitable role in the British Royal Air Force’s 242 Squadron, which was almost exclusively Canadian. There were also other additional RAF (Royal Air Force) squadrons comprising a few numbers of Canadians that helped provide much-needed support to prevent the terrifying threat posed by a potential German invasion. An unknown number of Canadians also served as groundcrew to help keep the fighters flying, who later received late, but well-warranted recognition in June of 1942.

In contrast, on the other side of the world, the Canadians at Hong Kong provided powerful support in the Battle of Hong Kong (1941), against Japanese aggression. It was the first time during the first sparks of the war that any Canadians had indulged in a land-battle. Canada contributed to the war effort at Hong Kong by deploying its Royal Rifles and the Winnipeg Grenadiers to help in the defense of Hong Kong, which was a British colony (1841-1997). However, the Japanese aggressors clearly demonstrated a potent superiority when it came to the number of personnel, battle experience, and access to reinforcements and resources, as well as total air domination. In a sound juxtaposition to this case, the Canadians (and the Allies) were relatively inexperienced, battle-exhausted from continual military exchanges and constant bombardment, as well starved from receiving additional supplies or reinforcements. The Canadians here suffered numerous casualties, and by the end, on Christmas day 1941, were forced to surrender to the torment of the enemy’s forces.

Another one of Canada’s most defining battles occurred during the largest seaborne invasion of modern history, otherwise known as the Invasion of Normandy of “D-Day” (1944). The Canadian assault force was composed of the 3rd Canadian Infantry Division and 2nd Canadian Armoured Brigade, which were a major denomination of the assault force at Juno Beach. Approximately 15,000 Canadian soldiers, in tandem with 10,000 Canadian sailors, and 450 aircrew participated in the raids conducted on June 6, 1944. The Royal Canadian Navy played a pivotal role in the battle, as it contributed 109 ships to the conflict, and helped deliver infantry and tanks directly onto the landing site known as Juno Beach. Furthermore, the Royal Canadian Air Force provided essential aerial support, by attacking the German ground forces, helped the Allies attain air supremacy early on. Last, but not least, Canadians repelled various German counterattacks on the following days, and were the only nation to meet all of its D-Day objectives, by managing to advance further than any other country into the German territory of France. In fact, Canadian soldiers were forced to turn back, in order to strengthen defensive positions far back. Ultimately, the Normandy campaign of 1944 resulted in over 18,444 casualties, and over 5,021 deaths for the Canadian Armed Forces.
